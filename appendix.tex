\section{Stratified Sampling}

When sampling snippets of specified length from the dataset, we use a stratified sampling approach.
We do this to avoid draws when many times a snippet gets sampled from a single region of the trial and dataset.
Such imbalance could lead to overfitting to a portion of a dataset and generally to treating of various regions disproportionately.
We study the effect of this approach and find out it may matter less than we anticipated.

We remind that the stratified approach means sampling in \( n_{\text{strata}} \) consecutive turns from \( n_{\text{strata}} \) disjoint regions of the sampled dataset.
The parameter \( n_{\text{strata}} \) controls the granularity or the number of disjoint regions summing up to the full trial length.
We do an experiment where we always sample from each trial a 100 times, but vary the number of strata \( n_{\text{strata}} \).
We test on the task of song identification in the MUSIN-G dataset.
The results are seen in the below graph and at most seem to suggest detrimental value of such sampling for use with the XGBoost model. 
The effect seems small enough to follow the intuition behind the stratified approach and use it either way.

\section{ICA}

In preprocessing of EEG data we use ICA as a dimension reduction transform on EEG signal.
ICA finds a linear transform of EEG signal into so called independent components which are ordered by importance.
Similarly to the use of PCA, we can drop bottom components and reduce number of channels of the EEG data.
We compare such use of ICA in preprocessing to instead picking a set of channels and dropping the rest.
The set of channels to be picked in this "No ICA" case is predefined and chosen based on proximity to auditory processing regions of the brain or a known relation to music processing.
