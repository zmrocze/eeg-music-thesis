\section{Song name prediction}

We start with a task of predicting the song name of a listened song, based on the EEG signal.
We split the MUSIN-G dataset into recordings of !!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!1 12 subjects as train and remaining 8 subjects as test (for CNN split half of test makes validation set).
We then train a model to predict the song name from the EEG signal.
We achieve the below results for each variant of the tested model.

    \hline
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\

    KNN     & 0.1374 \\
    SVM     & 0.1229 \\

    CNN      & 0.1740 \\
    \hline

  \caption{Song name prediction accuracy: subject-wise split.}
  \label{tab:song-prediction}

Our results are comparable to the study by Pandey and others\cite{pandey} in which researchers achieved 12.5\% mean subject accuracy (random classifier gives \(  \frac{1}{12} = 0.08(3) \))

In the above study researchers also tested scores achieved in a different testing paradigm. They split the MUSIN-G dataset into training and testing 
by taking initial \( n \) seconds of each recording as training data and remaining seconds as testing data. The study compares results that can be achieved for the few chosen \( n \) parameters (\(3, 5, 10, 20\)) achieving higher accuracy for bigger training partitions.
The results in this variant of the experiment are much higher (\( 84\% \)) than in the previous variant.
We are able to reproduce these results and for \( n=20 \) achieve accuracies like in the table:

  \label{tab:song-id-temporal-split}

To us this poor generalization suggests that the model in this second variant does not pick up on neural correlates of the music but rather on some trial-specific aspects of the EEG recording.
We test the hypothesis with two experiments. 

First, we run similar experiment on a the BCMI-training dataset. The dataset contains from 144 up-to 432 trials recorded for a single subject.
The recordings share between each other the ``emotionality'' of the listened music but no listened song repeats across the dataset as the songs are computer generated per each trial.
We obtain that, similarly to the study, classifiers are able with high accuracy to predict the trial id from the EEG signal.

\subsection{DTW-based trial id prediction}

Secondly, guided by the intuition that the classifiers notice directly repeatable patterns in the EEG signal between train and test and are not forced to generalize beyond that, we propose a given experiment.
We attempt to predict the trial id using DTW algorithm.

The DTW in the most basic form is a method of measuring a distance between two time series, by finding an optimal alignment between them.
Such alignment is a match between points of one time series and points of the other time series, that minimizes the sum of distances between matched points, 
starts and ends at the two matched ends of the two time series and is monotonic in between.
We employ a generalized version called approximate-subsequence-DTW, which can be defined by:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Given two sequences $A$ and $B$, it finds a match between an infix of $A$ and $B$.
  \item The summed distances between the matched points are within the cost allowance.
  \item The matched infix is of maximal length.
\end{enumerate}

For a single subject of BCMI-training dataset, we predict the trial id of a test trial by finding across all training trials,
the one that scores the highest when calculating the approximate-subsequence-DTW distance between the test trial and the training trial.
We fit the cost allowance hyperparameter on the validation set and then test the accuracy of the method on the test set. 
With this simple method we achive 50.83\% test accuracy.
% Accuracy: 61/120 = 50.83%
The experiment is meant to showcase that such direct pattern matching is possible and therefore --- it is possible for the model learn it and rely on it greatly, with little generalization capability.

\section{Emotion prediction}

BCMI-training dataset is made of computer-generated snippets of music. These musical snippets are tagged with emotionality labels, one of 9 possibilities aranged on a Valence and Arousal axis.
To compare the difficulty of this task with previous tasks, we can think of emotionality labels as abstraction classes joining multiple individual trials together.
Therefore, it is easier to predict an emotion label than an identifier as this requires less precision, but during the training the 
reward per training step is less valuable, gives less information.
The balance seems to not be in the favor of this task, as we are unable to achieve results, even though in the prediction task we are.

\section{Music ``density'' prediction}

Looking for musical features visible through the EEG recording we define a ``density'' of a music snippet and by extension the paired EEG snippet.
First, we mark note onsets in the music recordings. 
We do that by looking at the music's mel spectrograms and marking the time points in which the spectrogram's energy increases significantly, weighting the changes in the high frequencies more (High-Frequency-Content method).
The HFC method was visually compared to other methods and chosen as seemingly best.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{imgs/mel_onset_hvha1.wav.png}
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{imgs/mel_onset_hvha2.wav.png}
  \caption{Spectrograms of two 4s music snippets with marked note onsets as detected by the algorithm.}
  \label{fig:mel-onsets}
\end{figure}

Now, for a chosen length of a time window, we get a certain distribution on number of note onsets within windows of this length.
Calculating the median number, we can split the snippets into two classes: the ``sparse'' class with a number of onsets below the median and the ``dense'' class with a number of onsets above the median.
We calculate the median only on the training set and define a classification task of predicting the class of a snippet based on the paired EEG snippet.
We hope a success in this task may be a stronger indicator that some musical features are present and can be decoded from the EEG signal.
